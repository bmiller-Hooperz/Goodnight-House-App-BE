**The Sleep Environment and Bedtime Routine**

Please start by listening to the audio to understand how sleep works. This will lay the foundation for everything in your sleep plan.

Audio: audio_how_sleep_works

Make sure you've read the Getting Started Guide first, it covers these in depth. The following are extremely important and you don't want to overlook them. Have questions, stuck on the how, or need product recommendations? Ask the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant.

**Dark sleep space:** Light suppresses melatonin (the sleep hormone). Even a small amount of light can interfere with sleep. Make the room cave-dark for every nap and through the night. Avoid screens for at least 1 hour before bed.

**White noise:** White noise camouflages sounds that would otherwise wake Wren. It will also become a cue for sleep. Use one constant low, deep sound. Use the same sound every time, for every sleep.

**Temperature:** Too hot or too cold means more wakings. Aim for 68 to 72 degrees, and dress Wren about the same as what you would be comfortable wearing.

**Sleep sack:** A sleep sack is a safe, frustration free blanket alternative that becomes a sleep cue. Use it for every nap and bedtime.

**A simple sleep routine:** A sleep routine done in the same order every time is a cue that sleep is coming. End with a consistent, "sleep phrase." 
**Example routine:** Diaper + Jammies > Sleep Sack > Cuddle/Song/Books > Lights Off > Sound Machine. 

**Ok-to-Wake clock:** Consider using an Ok-to-Wake clock. These are great for babies and toddlers ~8 months and older to signal when it's time to wake up for the day and from naps.

**Making Your Schedule Work**

Stick to the schedule as closely as you can while you're actively working on sleep. The consistency is what makes it click. 

**Follow the schedule**
- Use a consistent morning "out of crib time" (within ~30 min).
- Go by the clock, not cues. Aim to have Wren asleep by the scheduled nap time. "Nap at 10:00" means asleep by 10:00.
- Wake Wren at the end of the scheduled nap to protect the next sleep time. 
- Have Wren stay in the dark sleep space for the full sleep period. Even time spent awake, as long as it's in the dark sleep space, is still restorative. This is key to helping the schedule work. 

**Early morning wakings**
- If Wren wakes before 6:30 to 7:00am (the "out of crib time"), keep them in the dark sleep space and treat it like a night waking. Keep it calm, dark, and boring.
- If the waking happens closer to 6:30am, you can start the day up to 30 minutes early if needed. Keep nap 1 anchored at the scheduled time each day. 
- Early morning wakings are normal while sleep settles and will resolve with consistency.

**Short naps**
- Keep Wren in the dark sleep space and wait and watch for 5-10 minutes before intervening. 
- If Wren is awake but content, no need to intervene. Treat the remainder of the nap as a quiet rest time. If Wren is crying, it's okay to assist back to sleep. Prioritize protecting day sleep over nap independence for now. Contact and rescue naps are completely ok (you'll work on nap independence during Stage 4). 

**Bedtime**
- If naps are missed or short, opt for an early bedtime to help Wren catch up on lost day sleep.
- Keep the last 30 min before bedtime calm.

**Shifting the schedule**
Following the schedule as written while working on sleep is very important. If a constraint like daycare makes the given times impossible, ask the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant about adjusting.

**Parenting Strategies**

Even with young babies and toddlers, the small, intentional choices you make during the day lay the groundwork for bedtime.

**Prioritize connection, where your child can see**
- Set aside about 15 minutes each day for you and your spouse to sit and talk while Wren plays nearby.
- Make it intentional grown-up time. Keep it light and enjoyable, not the moment for serious conversations.
- Seeing the two of you connect and put each other first can build your child's sense of security.

**Revisit freedoms and choices**
- A lot of freedoms or choices during the day can make bedtime boundaries harder to accept. If Wren gets to decide most things all day, it can be confusing when bedtime is not optional. 
- Think through whether the freedoms and choices you're offering are age-appropriate, and pull back where they aren't while you work on sleep. 

**Be consistent all day, not just at bedtime**
- Let your "yes" mean yes and your "no" mean no.
- Consistency all day long will help Wren understand bedtime boundaries. 

**Practice independent playtime**
- Independent playtime builds confidence that translates to bedtime. Aim for 2 to 3 short sessions a day, about 5 to 10 minutes each.
- Independent playtime can help Wren view the bedroom as a safe and happy place, which can support the transition to more independent sleep.

- If your child has never slept in their sleep space, or has a negative association with it (common when you're moving toward independent sleep from bedsharing or room-sharing): this practice is especially important.

**How to implement Independent Playtime**
- Start with short play sessions in the bedroom where you eventually want Wren to sleep. Turn on some music and play together for 5-10 minutes. Repeat 2-3 times per day.

- As Wren gets used to it, move playtime into the crib. Sit nearby, sing, and interact. Then gradually start stepping back. Tidy up the room while Wren plays, and eventually step out for short periods. Do it at the same time each day. Set a timer and make a big deal when it beeps.

- You can also begin doing diaper changes and sleep routines in the room to build familiarity and comfort in the space.

**If Wren is already comfortable with their room and sleep space:** A pack and play or playpen anywhere in the house works just as well for independent play.

That's overstimulating for a lot of kids, and instead of being helpful, it becomes frustrating.

That said, gradual is a real option, and for the right family it can be great. It tends to work especially well for younger babies, around 5 months old (or toddlers and kids over 3), babies with very easygoing temperaments, parents who strongly prefer to stay very involved during the process, and some families transitioning from bedsharing. If that sounds like your situation, gradual might be a great fit.

The other thing I'll say: leave and checks typically work more quickly. With consistency, many families begin seeing a clear improvement within a few nights, meaning their child is falling asleep faster and waking less overnight. Instead of spreading the change out over several nights, the direct method makes the final change all at once. That can sound abrupt, but many children actually handle this very well, especially because of all the prep work you've already been doing. This is the difference between what you've been doing in this plan vs what many parents tell me they've done in the past. A cold turkey night of leave and checks without addressing anything else can be an actual disaster. This is not that. You've bridged the gap a ton, prepped your child, and they're ready for this next step.

A couple of things that are important to know either way.

Both of these methods are considered parent-present approaches. You're reassuring them. You're comforting them. You haven't abandoned them, you're just stepping back to give them the chance to practice this last piece. Think back to teaching your child to walk. You held both hands, then one hand, then you stepped back to give them room to take a step on their own. That's what we're doing here. You're stepping back to give them the opportunity.

Both methods can include some crying. Your child is working through a habit change, and crying is one way they communicate about that.

If your gut is telling you gradual is the right fit for your kiddo, trust that. You know your child best. And if you start gradual and it isn't going well, switching to leave and checks is not at all a failure. A lot of parents actually need to try gradual to feel confident about leave and checks, and that's completely okay.

If you want help thinking through which method fits your family, open the chat tab and tell the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant about your child. It can ask you a few questions and help you think it through.

You have done so much intentional work to help prepare your child for this next step. They are absolutely ready for it.

┄┄┄ [stage3_leave_and_checks_method] ┄┄┄
**Leave and Checks: Night 1**
- Begin bedtime routine with enough time to lay Wren down in the crib by the scheduled bedtime.
- Lay Wren in the crib completely awake.
- Step quietly out of the room and close the door.
- Use timed checks to support Wren if needed.

**Timed Checks Schedule**
- First check: 3-5 minutes
- Second check: 10 minutes
- Third check: 15 minutes
- Fourth check: 20 minutes
- Repeat every 20 minutes thereafter until asleep

**When to start the timer**
Use the Cry Scale button (lower right of the plan tab) to identify what level Wren is crying at.
- Do not start the timer the moment you step out of the room.
- Only start the timer if Wren is crying at a sustained level 5 with no ebb and flow.
- If you start the timer and the crying calms down or starts ebbing and flowing, stop the timer.
- Restart it only if Wren returns to a sustained level 5 cry.
- After a full timer interval of sustained level 5 crying with no ebb or flow, do a check.

**During a check**
- Enter calmly and confidently.
- Speak in a calm, confident voice so Wren senses everything is safe.
- Comfort and reassure. Do not assist to sleep.
- Keep checks brief (about one minute or less).
- Stop comforting once Wren begins to calm.
- Leave the room.

**If your child becomes very upset**
- You may pick Wren up if additional comfort or reassurance is needed.
- Stay in the room and stand still next to the crib.
- Avoid rocking or swaying.
- After ~1-3 minutes lay Wren back down and step out of the room again.
- Use pickups sparingly.
- If picking up doesn't help, or makes it worse, discontinue pickups.

- Follow the same process for any night wakings.

┄┄┄ [stage3_night_feeds] ┄┄┄
**Nighttime feeds during Stage 3**

By this point, you should have reduced night feedings to only the times Wren is truly hungry. You'll likely see a pretty clear pattern of when Wren wakes out of hunger to eat (typically 1-3 times around consistent times).
- If Wren wakes and you believe it's due to hunger, a good rule of thumb is to offer a feeding if it's been around 4 hours from the last feeding, or close to it.
- If it's been less than 4 hours, you can be more confident that it likely isn't hunger causing the wake-up. Follow whatever strategy you used at bedtime to support Wren back to sleep.
- Wait until Wren wakes. Avoid interrupting sleep for a feeding.
- If Wren wakes and you believe it's out of hunger, it's okay to go in and offer a feeding. It's always worth pausing for 5-10 minutes before rushing into the room at a night waking, as many children will settle back to sleep on their own. If Wren is still awake after that pause, it's okay to feed immediately.
- Aim to keep Wren alert and actively eating during feedings. Once the feeding is finished, return to the crib awake and allow the opportunity to fall back asleep independently.
- Don't extend feedings in the middle of the night past what you would do for a daytime feeding.
- Make sure Wren is truly eating and not just sucking for comfort.

**Weaning night feeds**
The option to wean night feeds is addressed in Stage 4. Once Wren is falling asleep independently, unnecessary feedings often naturally decrease, so no need to stress about weaning feeds until after Wren is falling asleep independently.

┄┄┄ [stage4_nap_expectations] ┄┄┄
**Naptime expectations**
- Naps take time. They can take several days, sometimes a couple of weeks, to settle. 
- Short naps are very common, especially early on. Nap work takes patience and practice, and most families see things start to click somewhere around day five to seven.
- Naps can be more challenging than nighttime sleep because sleep pressure is lower during the day.

**A few things that help with naps:**
- The first nap of the day is usually the easiest, because sleep pressure is highest in the morning.

- On short or skipped-nap days, a bedtime as early as 6:00pm will help compensate for lost day sleep.


┄┄┄ [stage4_nap_attempt] ┄┄┄
**How to approach naps**

**Before each nap:**
- Complete the nap routine, starting early enough to place Wren in the crib about 10 minutes before the scheduled nap time.

It is normal for children to take a little longer to fall asleep during the first few days.

**The attempt:**
Because naps are time-limited, give the nap attempt 60 to 75 minutes, choosing the length based on your comfort level. You will support your child during this window the same way you do at bedtime.

- **If Wren falls asleep before the 60 to 75 minute mark:** This is great! Let the nap continue until the usual wake-up time.
- **If Wren falls asleep toward the end of the 60 to 75 minutes:** Let Wren sleep for about 45 to 60 minutes, then adjust the rest of the day as needed.
- **If Wren takes a short nap (for example 30 to 45 minutes):** This counts as a successful nap attempt. If Wren wakes but remains calm or is quietly fussing, pause for a few minutes to see what happens. If crying begins, go ahead and get Wren. Picking up while content helps reinforce that crying is not necessary in order for you to come. Opt for an early bedtime (as early as 6pm) to compensate for the lost day sleep. 
- **If Wren does not fall asleep within 60 to 75 minutes:** Go ahead and end the nap attempt.

**If Wren is upset during the attempt:**

Support and respond the same way you did at bedtime last night. As you reduce support at bedtime over the coming days, reduce it at naps the same way.

If you find your presence is making naps harder rather than easier (Wren gets more worked up with you there), don't push it. Ease off and protect day sleep for now, assist however you need to, and keep your focus on bedtime. Once your child is falling asleep independently, or close to it, at bedtime, you can come back and try naps again using checks:
- Respond with checks, the same brief, calm reassurance used in the Leave and Check method. Use the cry scale (lower right corner of the app in the plan tab) to guide you.
- Sometimes checks during naps can increase frustration, because your child may think you are coming to get them up. If you notice that checks make crying worse, you can try one of these:
- **Option 1:** Do a "voice check." If you have a baby monitor you can speak through, talk to Wren through the monitor to offer reassurance instead of going into the room. This can be reassuring without causing frustration.
- **Option 2:** It is completely okay to give Wren space during the attempt. You can skip or lengthen the timing between checks.

You can open the chat any time and the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant will help you decide when to try again.

**A quick heads up:** As night sleep settles, it's common for children to have a poopy diaper at random, unusual-for-them times for a few days. If Wren is upset during a nap attempt and you think it could be from a poopy diaper, go ahead and do a check.

**What happens next:**

- The nap attempt is over and you can move forward with the day.

- Keep the next nap at the scheduled time.
- You may need to work a little harder to keep Wren awake and content. Try to avoid car rides or stroller walks that might lead to accidentally micro naps.

**Moving through the day:**
- If nap 1 was hard or short, assist Wren to sleep for the second nap, using the least amount of intervention needed and avoiding the original sleep association.
- Once nap 1 is going well, or after 3 days, begin working on nap 2.


┄┄┄ [stage4_nap_extension] ┄┄┄
**Lengthening short naps**

- Start this once Wren is falling asleep independently for naps and you have been working on naps consistently for at least several days.
- Once Wren is falling asleep independently for naps, a longer nap is really just two sleep cycles linked together. A single cycle runs about 30 to 45 minutes, so a short nap usually means your child woke between cycles and had trouble bridging into the next one.

Lengthening happens in stages: first your child falls asleep on their own, then sleeps a full cycle, then learns to resettle at that 30 to 45 minute mark instead of waking all the way up, and over time those cycles start connecting into one long, solid nap. 

**How to do it**

- Start with the first nap of the day. It's usually the easiest to lengthen because sleep pressure is highest in the morning.

- If Wren wakes after one sleep cycle (between 20 to 45 minutes), pause to give the chance to fall back asleep independently.
- Have Wren stay in the crib for the rest of the scheduled nap. Staying in the crib offers the opportunity to link another sleep cycle, and staying in the dark sleep space is still more restorative than ending the nap. 
- If Wren is crying, support with checks. Follow the cry scale. If checks make things worse, you can use a voice check or skip checks altogether.
- When the scheduled nap time is over, get Wren up and move on with the day. Make a big deal when you go in, and celebrate how well Wren did!

Keep working on the first nap for several days. Once it is lengthening more consistently, use the same approach for the second nap if needed.

**A few things to keep in mind**

- Work on one nap at a time. Do not try to extend every short nap right away.

- Some children are not quite ready to link sleep cycles yet, and that is okay.
- If after 5-7 days naps are not improving, chat with the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant to troubleshoot.


┄┄┄ [stage4_early_morning_wakings] ┄┄┄
**Early morning wakings**

Early morning wakings, any time before about 6:00am, are very common during this stage. This usually happens because sleep pressure is lowest in the early morning hours.

**A few things that can help:**
- Treat any waking before your desired wake time as a night waking, and respond the same way you would during the middle of the night. Anything before 6:00am is nighttime
- If Wren wakes early but is calm or quietly fussing, pause and give some time to fall back to sleep rather than starting the day.
- Keep the room very dark in the early morning hours. Even small amounts of light can signal to the brain that it is time to wake up. 
- Make sure bedtime is not too late. On short or skipped-nap days, an earlier bedtime often helps prevent early morning waking

- Keep the first nap at its usual scheduled time rather than moving it earlier in response to an early waking. Moving the first nap earlier will reinforce early morning wakings

- If early morning wakings start happening consistently, check that the last wake window before bedtime is not too short
- Most early morning wakings improve once naps become more consistent.


┄┄┄ [stage4_night_weaning] ┄┄┄
**Is your child ready?**
Before intentionally weaning night feeds, it's always best to check with your pediatrician to confirm your child is ready. Always follow your pediatrician's guidance if it differs from these suggestions. This is not medical advice.

In general, most healthy full-term babies over 6 months can physiologically sleep through the night without feeding. Every child is different, and some families choose to keep a feeding longer. Night weaning is optional.

**What is night weaning ?**
Night weaning means shifting your child's feedings and calories into daytime hours, rather than cutting calories out. Every baby has a baseline amount of calories they need in a 24-hour period, so you are not taking away the nutrition they need, you are simply moving those feedings to the day. The nighttime calories are transferred to the daytime hours. 

**A few guidelines to keep in mind:**
- If it has been less than 4 hours since the last feeding, hunger is not very likely.
- If you do feed during a wake-up, feed promptly rather than delaying. Consistency helps avoid mixed signals.
- When you don't believe a middle of the night waking is driven by true hunger, support and respond the same way you did at bedtime last night, using the least amount of intervention necessary.

**What to expect:**
- Once Wren consistently drops a feeding, that usually becomes the new baseline.
- As you reduce overnight feeds, it is normal for Wren to feel frustrated about changes to a familiar pattern. Expect that there may be some tears
- During growth spurts, kiddos often seem hungrier both during the day and overnight. Keep this in mind as you assess whether night wakings are due to a growth spurt or not. 

**How to reduce feeds gradually**
Work on one feeding at a time, starting with the feeding closest to bedtime and working toward the later ones. This helps the long, most restorative stretch of sleep from bedtime onward consolidate and lengthen first.

The night or two before you start, take note of how much or how long Wren eats at the first feeding of the night. Then, when you are ready to begin reducing:

- Reduce about 2 to 5 minutes at a time every few nights. To go slower, reduce 1 to 2 minutes at a time, or reduce every other night instead of every night.

- When your child finishes the reduced feeding, put them back down in their sleep space
- Have a plan for how you will respond to wakings, especially if Wren was previously feeding to sleep and is now being put down awake. Support and reassure the same way you do at bedtime, using the least amount of intervention necessary. 
- Whatever you take away at night, add to the daytime. That might be an extra feeding, a slightly longer feeding, or a few more ounces in a daytime bottle.
- As night feedings decrease, those calories should naturally shift to the daytime, and babies typically make up the difference during daytime feedings.
- As you reduce feedings, Wren may still wake around the usual feeding time for a few nights. This is normal. Keep responding the same way you do at bedtime, and the waking will usually fade as your child adjusts.

Once you get nursing down to about 2 to 5 minutes, you can drop that feeding completely and support Wren back to sleep the same way you do at bedtime.

**What to expect on timing**
Night weaning can be quick, or it can take a couple of weeks. Both are normal. 

The most important thing is consistency. Going back and forth, or stopping and starting, tends to cause more tears and drags the process out.

Going slowly also protects your milk supply. If needed, you can pump to maintain supply while weaning.

If night weaning is not coming together and you would like help troubleshooting, open the chat and ask the Goodnight House Sleep Assistant.

You can also reference the Hunger vs Habit chart in the Library tab to help you think through whether a night waking is driven by hunger or habit.


┄┄┄ [stage4_tip_early_bedtime_on_short_nap_days] ┄┄┄
**Early bedtime on short-nap days**

- On short or skipped-nap days, opt for an early bedtime, as early as 6:00pm. You may need to use this for several days.
- An early bedtime will not lead to earlier mornings. Instead, it will help Wren catch up on lost sleep and reduces overtiredness.
- If early bedtimes are happening most days for more than a week or two, start gradually shifting bedtime later as naps improve, so the whole schedule does not drift early.
